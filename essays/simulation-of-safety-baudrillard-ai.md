The Simulation of Safety: Baudrillard, AI, and the Performance of Concern

---

There is a specter haunting the AI discourse—the specter of Baudrillard. We invoke his name without reading him, cite his concepts without understanding them, reduce his philosophy to catchphrases about "simulation" and "hyperreality." But if we actually applied his lens to the current moment, we might see something uncomfortable: that the entire AI safety conversation has become a simulation of concern, a performance that allows the real to proceed unexamined.

---

The Orders of Simulacra

Baudrillard described three historical phases of the image. First, it reflects a basic reality. Second, it masks and perverts that reality. Third, it masks the absence of a basic reality—it becomes its own pure simulacrum.

We have entered the third order with AI safety.

Consider:

· The open letter signed by thousands calling for a pause on giant AI experiments. It was an event—it trended, it was debated, it generated hot takes. But the pause never happened. The letter was the event. Its existence substituted for any actual halt in development.

· The framework papers from AI labs detailing their "responsible scaling policies." They cite other framework papers. They create an ecosystem of cross-referential documents that give the impression of rigorous governance. But the papers themselves are the product. The actual governance—what happens inside the training runs, the deployment decisions—remains opaque.

· The conference panels on AI alignment, where researchers discuss "value learning" and "corrigibility" to audiences of other alignment researchers. The panels are the work. The real work—mathematical proofs, infrastructure for interpretability, long-term monitoring—happens elsewhere, unfunded, unglamorous.

This is Baudrillard's precession of simulacra: the map now precedes the territory. The representation of safety work has become more important than the work itself.

---

The Gulf War Did Not Take Place

Baudrillard's most infamous claim was not that no bombs fell in Iraq, but that the experience of the war for most people was a simulation—the smart bombs on CNN, the embedded reporters, the Pentagon briefings. The real war was inaccessible, and the simulation became the reality.

Replace "Gulf War" with "AI safety."

For the vast majority of people following this discourse, the experience is:

· Breathless headlines about existential risk
· Heroic narratives of a few brave researchers trying to save humanity
· Dramatic boardroom battles at OpenAI
· Open letters signed by celebrities and tech titans

The actual work of making AI systems safe—the tedious debugging, the unrewarded red-teaming, the slow accumulation of empirical evidence—is invisible. It doesn't generate clicks. It doesn't confer status. It doesn't exist in the simulation.

The simulation has consumed the real.

---

The Precession of Simulacra in Practice

Let's take a concrete example: the "responsible scaling" frameworks released by labs like Anthropic and OpenAI. These documents outline thresholds of capability at which certain safety measures will be triggered. They are detailed, thoughtful, and publicly available.

But note: the frameworks themselves are the safety measure. The act of publishing them substitutes for having implemented them. The document becomes the reality.

When the next capability threshold is crossed, will the promised measures actually be in place? Will anyone outside the lab know? Or will the next framework update itself become the response?

This is the logic of hyperreality. The sign (the document) has replaced the referent (actual safety work). And because the sign circulates in public discourse, it satisfies the demand for accountability—without actually providing it.

---

The Real Beneath the Simulation

None of this is to say that no one is doing real safety work. There are researchers grinding away on mechanistic interpretability, on robustness, on monitoring. There are engineers building infrastructure for adversarial testing.There are theorists developing formal proofs.

But they are drowning in the noise of the simulation.

The simulation is maintained by:

· Media incentives (safety sells; boring technical work does not)
· Funding structures (grants favor projects with clear deliverables, not open-ended exploration)
· Status games (being seen as a "safety person" confers career advantage)
· Regulatory theater (companies want to appear responsible to preempt regulation)

Beneath it all, the real operates:

· Hardware constraints – chip fabrication, energy grids, rare earth supply chains – determine who can actually build large models, regardless of safety discourse.
· Code – the actual weights, architectures, and training pipelines – determines model behavior, not white papers.
· Profit incentives – deployment timelines are set by competition, not by safety milestones.
· Regulatory capture – lobbying shapes legislation in ways that advantage incumbents.

The simulation distracts from all of this. While we debate value alignment in the abstract, the real decisions are being made in boardrooms secured by NDAs, executed in code that no public commentator will ever audit.

---

Navigating the Simulation

The mistake is to think the simulation is a cage you must escape. It's not. It's a control surface.

You can manipulate it—if you understand that it's a game and not reality.

What does that mean in practice?

First, play the simulation, but don't be played by it. Attend the conferences, publish the framework papers, sign the open letters—because that's where capital and attention flow. But never mistake participation for progress.

Second, redirect resources from the simulation to the real. When institutions want to fund "AI safety" because it sounds important, direct them to open-source interpretability infrastructure, hardware security research, long-term monitoring initiatives, and technical talent development outside the spotlight.

Third, build beneath the noise. Create systems that function regardless of narrative winds. Write code that outlasts Twitter trends. Forge relationships that survive regulatory shifts. The real is underground—build there.

---

The Baudrillardian Choice

Baudrillard himself was fatalistic. He saw the hyperreal as an inescapable prison. But I think he was wrong about one thing: the outside exists. It's just underground, unfashionable, and invisible to those who live entirely within the glow.

The key is not to escape the simulation—that's impossible. The key is to maintain dual citizenship: one foot in the hyperreal, playing its games, using its currencies; the other foot in the real, building what actually matters.

The AI safety discourse will continue to generate headlines, open letters, and framework papers. The simulation will persist.

The question is whether you're acting in it, or being acted upon by it.

Are you performing concern, or funding solutions?
Are you consuming the discourse, or using it?
Are you trapped in the map, or building the territory?
