The Simulation of Safety: Baudrillard, AI, and the Performance of Concern

There's a specter haunting AI discourse. Baudrillard. We throw his name around like we know what it means. We don't. We quote "simulation" and "hyperreality" like they're magic words. But if we actually looked through his lens at what's happening right now, we'd see something uncomfortable. The whole AI safety conversation has become a performance. A simulation of concern that lets the real stuff happen in the dark.

Baudrillard said images go through three phases. First they reflect reality. Then they mask it. Then they mask the fact that there's no reality left at all. The image becomes its own thing. Pure simulacrum.

We're deep in phase three with AI safety.

Remember that open letter? Thousands of signatures calling for a pause on giant AI experiments. It trended. People debated it. Hot takes everywhere. But here's the thing. The pause never happened. The letter WAS the event. Its existence substituted for any actual halt in development. The sign replaced the thing it was supposed to represent.

Look at the framework papers coming out of labs like Anthropic and OpenAI. "Responsible scaling policies." They cite each other. Cross-reference back and forth until you have this ecosystem of documents that looks like rigorous governance. But the papers ARE the product. The actual governance, what happens inside training runs, deployment decisions, who gets to see what, remains completely opaque. Nobody outside knows. The documents satisfy the demand for accountability without providing any.

Then there are the conference panels. Researchers talking about "value learning" and "corrigibility" to audiences full of other alignment researchers. The panels ARE the work. Meanwhile the real work, mathematical proofs, infrastructure for interpretability, long-term monitoring, happens somewhere else. Unfunded. Unglamorous. Invisible.

This is what Baudrillard meant by precession of simulacra. The map comes before the territory. The representation of safety work matters more than the work itself.

Baudrillard got famous for saying the Gulf War didn't take place. People thought he was denying bombs fell on Iraq. He wasn't. He was saying that for most people, the war WAS the CNN footage. The smart bombs. The embedded reporters. The Pentagon briefings. The real war was inaccessible. So the simulation became the reality.

Replace "Gulf War" with "AI safety."

For most people following this stuff, the experience is breathless headlines about existential risk. Heroic narratives about brave researchers saving humanity. Drama at OpenAI board meetings. Celebrity signatures on open letters. That's what AI safety IS to them.

The actual work, tedious debugging, red-teaming that nobody rewards, slow accumulation of empirical evidence, that's invisible. Doesn't generate clicks. Doesn't confer status. Might as well not exist.

The simulation ate the real.

Take "responsible scaling" frameworks. Labs publish these detailed documents about capability thresholds where safety measures trigger. Thoughtful. Publicly available. But the frameworks themselves ARE the safety measure. Publishing them substitutes for implementing them. The document becomes the reality.

When the next capability threshold hits, will the promised measures actually be in place? Will anyone outside the lab even know? Or will they just release another framework update and call it a response?

That's hyperreality. The sign replaced the referent. And because the sign circulates in public discourse, everyone feels like accountability happened. But it didn't.

I'm not saying nobody does real safety work. People grind away on mechanistic interpretability. Robustness. Monitoring. Engineers build adversarial testing infrastructure. Theorists develop formal proofs. They're out there.

But they're drowning in noise.

The simulation is kept alive by media incentives, safety sells but boring technical work doesn't. Funding structures that want clear deliverables, not open-ended exploration. Status games where being seen as a "safety person" helps your career. Regulatory theater where companies act responsible to avoid actual regulation.

Underneath all that noise, the real keeps operating. Hardware constraints, chip fabrication, energy grids, rare earth supply chains, determine who can actually build large models regardless of what the safety discourse says. Code determines model behavior, not white papers. Profit incentives set deployment timelines, not safety milestones. Lobbying shapes legislation to advantage incumbents.

The simulation distracts from all of this. We debate value alignment in the abstract while real decisions get made in boardrooms behind NDAs, executed in code nobody outside will ever audit.

Here's the thing though. The simulation isn't a cage you need to escape. It's a control surface. You can work it if you understand it's a game.

Play the simulation but don't let it play you. Go to the conferences. Publish the framework papers. Sign the open letters. That's where capital and attention flow. Just don't mistake participation for progress.

Redirect resources from the simulation to the real. When institutions want to fund "AI safety" because it sounds important, point them to open-source interpretability tools. Hardware security research. Long-term monitoring. Technical talent development that happens outside the spotlight.

Build beneath the noise. Create systems that work regardless of narrative winds. Write code that outlasts Twitter trends. Relationships that survive regulatory shifts. The real is underground. Build there.

Baudrillard was a fatalist. He thought the hyperreal was an inescapable prison. He was wrong about one thing. The outside exists. It's just underground. Unfashionable. Invisible to people who live entirely in the glow.

You can't escape the simulation. That's not the move. The move is dual citizenship. One foot in the hyperreal, playing the game, using its money. Other foot in the real, building what actually matters.

The AI safety discourse will keep generating headlines. Open letters. Framework papers. The simulation isn't going anywhere.

The question is whether you're acting in it or being acted upon by it.

Are you performing concern or funding solutions? Consuming the discourse or using it? Trapped in the map or building the territory?

These ideas belong to whoever finds them useful. Baudrillard probably wouldn't approve, but he's not around to object.
